Latin

Latin is a classical language, primarily used by the now-extinct Roman Empire. As a classical language, its history stretches very far back with an estimated ~2700 years since its inception. Once the lingua franca (common languange) of the Roman Empire, it was reduced to the common language for educated peoples. Today, Latin is considered a dead language, with no primary speakers and no native country or peoples. However, Latin was and continues to be used as the foremost language in many aspects of science and religion.

Latin originates from the lower Tiber area in Italy, called Latium, around Rome. This area was the roots of the Roman Empire. Latin then spread along as the Empire expanded, leaving Latin as the sole language for education. After the Roman Empire split and collapsed, many intellectuals continued to use Latin for the next ~1500 years.

Latin is divided into 2 types: Vulgar Latin, and Ecclesiastical Latin. Vulgar Latin was used as the main spoken language, while ecclasiastical Latin was used for official government functions, religious texts, and mainly by highly-educated aristocrats.
